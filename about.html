Design Process

The first thing I did to begin designing this project was look up different visuals of the London Cholera Map to get a better feel for what it should look like. Since the data for the project is essentially a lot of line segments I knew it wouldn't look exactly like the original Cholera map so I wanted to set the right expectations for the project. This step was extremely helpful as it also gave me ideas for how I could layout the whole page including the map and additional graphs.

After the first sketch I decided to start working on creating the map and the graphs as I assumed this step would take the longest, and I wanted to make sure I could successfully make each visualization before I started worrying about styling the page. In retrospect this might have been a mistake because once I was ready to start styling the page I ended up creating a whole new html file in order to be more deliberate about the divs I was creating to fit the layout I wanted. This also lead to me having to tweak my initial sketch.

Design Choices

When deciding on a color palette I wanted to use colors that were bright enough that they could be easily differentiated, but also make sure that they were somewhat easy to look at. To do this I used the website 'coolors.co' which is a random palette generator. Once I settled on a palette I liked, I ran it through a color blindness similator (color-blindness.com) to make sure it would be acceptable in that regard.

Next I decided where to apply the colors I had chosen:

The next major design choice was the layout of the graphs. I knew immediately that I wanted the Timeline graph to be next to the map since it includes interactivity with the map so that just makes the most sense. I ultimately decided to include instructional / information text above both the map and the Timeline graph because I wanted to have them to appear to have the same dimensions visually, and I didn't like that it looked uneven with only the map information. It also just makes sense to include instructions for the timeline graph as it is also interactive. This just left the remaining two graphs which made sense to layout next to each other.

The last major design choice I made was how to design the map visualization as it has many points on it which can easily become overwhelming and hard to read. I carefully chose the size of the death circles to be big enough to still distinguish, but not so big that they become a jumbled mess. I also decided to have them grow larger when you hover over them to emphasize which circle's information is being shown above. As the deaths are the visual I wanted to emphasize on the map, I chose to make the other elements (the pumps, buildings, and major street names) black and white. I just made sure to make them big enough to be distinguishable, and also included changes in color or size when you hover over them to further distinguish them. Lastly, I made the deaths highlighted by the Timeline graph the red color and increased their size so they could easily pop against the rest of the map.

Discoveries & Questions

Before beginning this project I had some initial questions:

  • What does the map tell us about where the infection was coming from?
  • How quickly did the outbreak spread?
  • Was there a significant difference in deaths by gender?
  • Was there a significant difference in deaths by age group? Did it affect older or younger people more?
After completing the visualizations these questions were easy to answer:
  • The most obvious discovery from the map visualation is that you can easily see that the pump in the middle next to the brewery likely had the infected water, as a huge majority of the deaths are clustered in that general area.
  • Another interesting thing to note is how quickly the deaths shot up and then went down. The whole outbreak only lasted the span of a couple of months but yet caused the deaths of over 500 people.
  • It is clear that Cholera does not discriminate based on gender, as the gender split of deaths is almost completely even.
  • As I suspected, age did affect the outcomes. The age groups greatest affected were children and the elderly.
